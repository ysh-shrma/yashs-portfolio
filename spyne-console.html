The Problem

As we scaled Vini, Spyne's conversational AI agent for US auto dealerships, to 30+ rooftops, the agents were doing real work: handling calls, booking appointments, running outbound campaigns. But dealers had no way to see the value being created. The ROI was invisible, which made renewals harder and expansion conversations vague.

The console experience was long overdue for a redesign, which kept getting pushed back to focus on AI agent quality and better outcomes for the dealers.

As the PM, I took this on end-to-end: not just defining how it should work, but building the functional prototype myself without a designer and validating the designs directly with dealers.

How I Validated It

I didn't design in isolation. I put the prototype in front of real dealers and let their feedback reshape the product. The wireframes weren't designed from assumptions: the customer conversations directly informed what went on the screen.

"If you had to meet your GM tomorrow morning and present that the agent is actually doing some real work: is this good enough?"
My question to a BDC manager during console validation. She immediately started doing live math on the screen: 127 appointments × 80% show rate = 101 walk-ins × 30% close rate = 34 cars sold. That told me two things: the funnel metrics strip was the right format, and the numbers had to survive a dealer doing mental math on the spot.
"That's the stuff that makes salespeople's heads spin. They just need to be served up on a platter: do this next. To sell a car."
An industry veteran during the console demo. This directly shaped how I redesigned the action items experience: away from a flat list of every call's follow-up, toward a prioritised queue with one-click actions. A salesperson should open this page and know exactly what to do next without reading a transcript.
"How many appointments set, how many showed, how many sold? That's the most important thing. That's what I look at before I sign up for another month."
A multi-rooftop dealer GM. And then the critical qualifier: "Customers just say yes to an appointment to get you off the phone. So the actual conversations of 'yes, I am interested': that's what matters." He was warning us about vanity metrics in our own product. This is why the dashboard leads with a full-funnel Leads by Source table that traces every lead from source to sold, broken down by channel.

What These Conversations Shaped

Overview Dashboard

The top strip shows five numbers at a glance: leads interacted, leads qualified, after-hours leads, human handoffs, and appointments booked. This is the "10-second check" the BDC manager validated: is the agent working or not?

  • Speed-to-Lead panel: Hero metric is average first contact time with week-over-week comparison. Four supporting metrics: leads instantly reached, leads engaged, appointments from instant response, and % of CRM leads captured
  • Leads by Source table: Full funnel by source (internet, walk-in, phone, other) across leads, connect rate, appointments, visits, show rate, sold, and close rate. The close rate column is separated with a dashed border to visually distinguish "our work" from "dealership work"

Action Items (Redesigned)

The old action items page was a flat list of "Reach out to customer" items on every call: unusable noise. The redesign, directly shaped by the "serve it on a platter" feedback:

  • Tabs by status (Open, In Progress, Completed) with counts
  • Each card shows customer name, typed action (e.g., "Schedule Test Drive," "Trade-In Follow-Up"), urgency indicator, and due date
  • Click-through opens a slide-out with full context: conversation summary, customer preferences, vehicle of interest, and a one-click action
